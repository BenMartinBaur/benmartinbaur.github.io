# 🧠 Michael Review — "Leading Without Authority"

**Reviewer:** Michael (Leadership Mindset Coach)
**Post:** Leading Without Authority — What 15 Years Taught Me About Influence
**Author:** Ben Martin Baur
**Date Reviewed:** 2026-04-23
**Pillar:** Real (🎤 Honest Reflections)

---

## 1. Authenticity Score: 4.5 / 5

This is the real thing. The post opens not with a win but with a *silence* — "the silence afterwards was deafening" — and that is a writer who has actually sat with a failure long enough to let it teach him. The admission of being "politely ignored" is genuinely vulnerable; most leadership writers would skip straight to the turnaround. Ben stays in the discomfort.

Where I hold back the final half-point: the middle section recounts an impressively clean arc — listen, grow, win Pinnacle Award. Real growth is messier than that. I'd love to see one moment where the listening approach *didn't* work, or a relationship that never came around. The narrative is honest, but it's still a little too tidy in its resolution. Vulnerability about the *ongoing* struggle — not just the past one — would push this to a 5.

---

## 2. Empowerment Check: ✅ Strong

This post passes the critical test: *Does the reader close the tab thinking "I could do that"?*

Yes. And here's why it works — Ben doesn't attribute his success to talent, charisma, or some innate leadership gene. He attributes it to a behaviour anyone can adopt: **asking "What's the hardest thing on your plate right now?"** That single question is concrete, repeatable, and free of jargon. A 25-year-old IC reading this can use it on Monday morning.

The four principles in "What I Learned the Hard Way" are all framed as *practices*, not *traits*. That's the difference between empowering writing and intimidating writing. "Credibility is the only currency that compounds" is not "be more credible" — it's a mental model the reader can operationalise.

The privilege acknowledgment in "One More Thing" is also crucial for empowerment. By naming his access and institutional support, Ben avoids the survivorship-bias trap that makes so many leadership posts feel hollow to people in harder contexts.

---

## 3. Psychological Safety: ✅ Strong, with one note

The writing creates real psychological safety for the reader. Three mechanisms are at work:

1. **Ben fails first.** The reader watches the author stumble before they're asked to absorb any lesson. This is textbook modelling of psychological safety — the leader goes first into the vulnerable space.

2. **No prescriptive language.** The post uses "Here's what I learned" not "Here's what you should do." This maps directly to the brand voice guide ("Inclusive: 'Here's what I've learned' not 'Here's what you're doing wrong'") and it matters — it keeps the reader's defences down.

3. **The closing invitation.** "If any of this resonates — or if you've learned different lessons on the same path — I'd love to hear your story" — this is an explicit bid for dialogue, not a mic drop. It signals that the author's story is *one* story, not *the* story.

**The one note:** The Pinnacle Award mention (43 out of 228,000+) is handled carefully — Ben frames it as an outcome of the team's work — but the specificity of the stat creates a brief power imbalance. The reader shifts for a moment from "I'm learning alongside this person" to "this person is in a different league." It's not disqualifying, but it's a moment where safety dips slightly. See Recommendation #2 below.

---

## 4. Growth Mindset Signals: ✅ Present throughout

This post is a growth mindset case study. The signals:

- **Learning from failure is the engine of the narrative.** The entire structure is: I tried → it failed → I examined why → I adjusted → it worked. That's the growth mindset loop made concrete.

- **"I didn't learn these from a framework. I learned them from getting it wrong, adjusting, and trying again."** (Line 71) — This is a direct, un-hedged articulation of learning-through-iteration. It also implicitly gives the reader permission to learn the same way.

- **"I still lead without formal authority every single day"** (Line 120) — This frames leadership-without-authority as an ongoing practice, not a stage he's graduated from. That's critical. It says: mastery is a direction, not a destination.

- **The self-advice section** ("What I'd Tell a Younger Version of Myself") models the growth mindset beautifully — it implies the author is a different person now than he was then, and that the transformation came through deliberate reflection, not time alone.

**What's missing:** A moment of *current* uncertainty. The post acknowledges past mistakes generously, but the present tense is confident and resolved. One line about something he's *still getting wrong* — even a small thing — would signal that the growth mindset isn't just retrospective.

## 5. Strongest Moment

> *"He talked for forty minutes. He told me about a customer engagement that was falling apart, about internal processes that made his job harder, about ideas he'd been sitting on because nobody had asked. By the end of that call, I hadn't solved a single problem. But I'd done something more important — I'd shown up as someone who actually gave a damn."*
> — Lines 53–55

**Why this is the peak:** This is the moment the entire post exists to deliver, and it earns every word. It works because:

1. **It's specific.** We're not told "I started listening." We see the forty minutes, the customer engagement, the unasked ideas. Specificity is the language of truth.
2. **It reverses the power dynamic.** The leader becomes the student. The person with the mandate learns from the person in the field. That inversion is what makes influence-based leadership feel different from authority-based leadership.
3. **"Nobody had asked" is devastating.** Four words that indict every leader who has ever broadcast a vision without doing discovery first. This phrase will stick with readers long after they close the tab.
4. **It models the exact behaviour it advocates.** The post doesn't just *tell* you to listen — it *shows* you what listening looks like and what it produces. That's writing that teaches by demonstration.

---

## 6. Weakest Moment

> *"Today, as a Technical Architect in the Innovation Hub, I still lead without formal authority every single day. I work across teams, across functions, across customer organisations. No one has to listen to me. The ones who do, do so because — I hope — they trust that I'm bringing something honest to the table."*
> — Lines 120–121

**Why this loses its edge:** After the raw vulnerability of the opening and the earned wisdom of the middle, this closing section shifts into a tone that feels like a polished LinkedIn summary. "Technical Architect in the Innovation Hub" reads as title-signalling in a post that argues titles don't matter. "No one has to listen to me" is slightly performative humility — the preceding paragraphs have just established that a lot of people do, in fact, listen.

The "I hope" hedge is nice, but it's doing the work that a *specific, present-tense moment of uncertainty* should be doing. Compare this to the opening: the opening gives us a scene, a feeling, a silence. The closing gives us a position statement. The post deserves a closing that matches the emotional specificity of its beginning.

## 7. Specific Recommendations

### Recommendation 1: Add one moment of current struggle (Growth Mindset)
**Where:** Between the "Four Things" section and "What I'd Tell a Younger Version of Myself" (after Line 95)

The post is honest about past failures but presents the current state as resolved. Add 2–3 sentences about something you're *still* navigating. Example territory: a cross-team dynamic that isn't working, a relationship you haven't cracked, a habit you keep falling back into. This turns the growth arc from past→present into past→present→ongoing, which is more truthful and more useful to the reader.

### Recommendation 2: Soften the Pinnacle Award stat (Psychological Safety)
**Where:** Lines 62–63

> *"In 2025, the impact of that work was part of why I received the Microsoft Pinnacle Award — an award given to 43 people out of 228,000+ employees."*

The "43 out of 228,000+" specificity creates an unintended status spike. Consider reframing to emphasise the team's work more and the stat less:

> *"In 2025, the ripple effects of that work were recognised with a Microsoft Pinnacle Award — something I still see as a reflection of what the team built, not anything I did alone."*

This keeps the achievement visible (which matters for credibility) but recentres the team and lowers the status differential with the reader.

### Recommendation 3: Rewrite the closing with a scene, not a summary (Authenticity)
**Where:** Lines 118–126 ("The Invitation" section)

Replace the current position-statement opening with a *moment* — the way the post opens with a moment. For example: a recent call where you caught yourself doing the old broadcast-tower thing, or a coffee conversation where someone challenged your thinking. End the post the way you started it — in a specific, human scene — and *then* extend the invitation. The coffee emoji is warm; give it a scene to land in.

### Recommendation 4: Add a "try this" micro-action (Empowerment)
**Where:** After the four principles (around Line 95) or in the closing

The four lessons are strong mental models, but the post could be even more empowering with one concrete "try this Monday" prompt. Something like:

> *"If you're leading without authority right now, here's the smallest thing I can recommend: pick one person on your team you haven't really talked to. Call them. No agenda. Ask them what's hard right now. Then just listen."*

This converts the reader's resonance into action. It's also a callback to the post's most powerful moment, which reinforces it.

### Recommendation 5: One sentence of present-tense imperfection in the closing (Vulnerability)
**Where:** Line 121, near "I hope"

The "I hope" is doing a lot of work. Make it explicit:

> *"I still get it wrong. I still catch myself sending the strategy doc before making the phone call. But I've learned that the correction is always the same: slow down, call someone, and ask what's hard."*

This keeps the ending humble without being performative, and it circles back to the core lesson.

## 8. Overall Verdict: ✅ Ready to Publish (with minor revisions)

This is a genuinely strong piece of leadership writing. It does something rare: it makes influence-based leadership feel *accessible* rather than aspirational. The opening scene is one of the best things on this blog. The four principles are clear, earned, and actionable. The privilege acknowledgment is mature and necessary.

The revisions I'm recommending are not structural — they're about closing the gap between a very good post and an exceptional one. Specifically:

- **Add one thread of present-tense imperfection** (Recommendations 1 & 5) to prevent the narrative from feeling too resolved
- **Rewrite the closing as a scene** (Recommendation 3) to match the emotional specificity of the opening
- **Add a micro-action** (Recommendation 4) to convert resonance into behaviour change
- **Soften one stat** (Recommendation 2) to maintain psychological safety throughout

None of these require rethinking the post. They require 20 minutes of revision. The bones are excellent. The voice is exactly right for the Real pillar — honest, inclusive, experience-led, not prescriptive.

**Ben, this is your best Real pillar piece so far.** The 40-minute phone call scene is going to stay with people. Publish it — just give the ending the same care you gave the beginning.
