## Why This Exists

Here's a pattern I see in almost every customer engagement: the workshop is booked, the room is full, the whiteboard is ready — and the first 90 minutes get burned on context-setting because nobody aligned on the problem beforehand.

Business Envisioning workshops are one of the most powerful tools in the [Microsoft Innovation Hub](https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/microsoft-cloud/dev/copilot/isv/business-envisioning) toolkit. They bring IT leaders, service owners, and business stakeholders together to identify use cases, score them against Business‑Experience‑Technology (BXT) dimensions, and leave with a prioritised backlog. But they only work if people arrive prepared.

So I started sending a preparation questionnaire to every customer before the workshop. Not a survey — a structured thinking exercise. It forces participants to reflect on what's working, what failed, where the opportunity is, and who owns the outcomes. By the time we're in the room, we're not discovering the landscape — we're already navigating it.

Below is the questionnaire I use. Adapt it to your context. The format doesn't matter — bullet points are fine. What matters is that people think before they talk.

---

## The Questionnaire

> **Audience:** IT Managers, Service Owners, and Business Stakeholders
>
> **Context:** Pre-workshop preparation for a Business Envisioning engagement
>
> **Purpose:** Ensure we use workshop time to focus on the right problems, shared priorities, and clear decisions.

---

### 1️⃣ Current State — Reality Check

**1.1 What's working well today?**

- What aspects of your IT infrastructure work reliably today?
- Where do you see clear business value or positive user feedback?
- Which platforms, services, or processes are enabling speed, stability, or efficiency?
- What would you explicitly *not* want to lose or replace?

**1.2 Current blockers & friction**

- Where do you experience the biggest blockers or bottlenecks?
- What slows delivery, innovation, or decision‑making?
- Where do dependencies, silos, or handovers create friction?
- What repeatedly frustrates teams or business stakeholders?

---

### 2️⃣ Learning from the Past — What Didn't Work (and Why)

**2.1 Projects that failed or stalled**

- Which initiatives failed to deliver the expected value?
- At what stage did they break down? (idea, design, delivery, adoption)
- What were the main root causes?
  - Unclear ownership or sponsorship
  - Missing business alignment
  - Change resistance
  - Technical complexity
  - Governance, security, or compliance delays

> *Be honest — this is about learning, not blame.*

**2.2 What success actually required**

- Which initiatives *did* work well?
- What made them successful?
- What conditions were present that are often missing?
  - Clear decision authority
  - Strong business sponsor
  - Simple scope
  - Empowered teams
  - Solid data foundations

---

### 3️⃣ Opportunity — Where to Focus Next

**3.1 Biggest opportunity areas**

- Where do you see the highest potential business impact in the next 12–24 months?
- Which processes, services, or capabilities:
  - Cost too much today?
  - Scale poorly?
  - Limit speed or quality?
  - Create operational or compliance risk?

*Optional prompts: operations, supply chain, data & analytics, employee productivity, customer experience, security & governance*

**3.2 If constraints disappeared…**

- If today's constraints did not exist, what would you redesign first?
- What would "great" look like for your IT organisation?
- What should IT be known for in three years?

---

### 4️⃣ Vision — Your Perspective

- What is your personal vision for your organisation's IT?
- What should fundamentally change?
- What should intentionally stay the same?

Finish this sentence:

> *"In three years, IT should enable the business by …"*

---

---

### 6️⃣ Constraints & Dependencies

- Are there non‑negotiable constraints we must consider?
  - Security
  - Compliance
  - Data privacy
  - Architecture standards
- Are there critical dependencies on other teams, vendors, or partners?

---

### 7️⃣ Decision & Ownership Readiness

- Who needs to be involved for decisions to stick?
- Who would own outcomes if we move forward?
- Where do decisions typically get delayed today?

## How the Input Gets Used

Responses don't just disappear into a slide deck. Here's exactly what happens with them:

1. **Theme extraction** — I look for shared patterns, blockers, and opportunities across all responses
2. **Rose–Thorn–Bud analysis** — What's working (roses), what's painful (thorns), what has potential (buds)
3. **BXT scoring** — Use cases get evaluated on Business viability, Experience impact, and Technology feasibility
4. **Prioritised backlog** — The workshop ends with a ranked list of use cases and clear next steps

The questionnaire turns a 4-hour workshop into a 4-hour *decision-making session*. That's the difference.

---

## Tips for Respondents

- **Bullet points are sufficient** — don't write essays
- **Be specific and concrete** — "our approval process takes 3 weeks" beats "things are slow"
- **Focus on problems and outcomes** — not solutions (yet)
- **Be honest about failures** — the best workshops happen when people stop protecting past decisions

## Why I Keep Using This

I've run this questionnaire with enterprise customers across industries — retail, manufacturing, financial services, public sector. The questions barely change. The answers always surprise me.

The most valuable thing it does isn't information gathering — it's **alignment**. When five IT leaders independently write down what they think the biggest opportunity is, and three of them say the same thing, you walk into the workshop with momentum instead of debate.

And when they *don't* agree? That's even more valuable. Now you know where the real conversation needs to happen.
