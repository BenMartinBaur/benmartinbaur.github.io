# Leading Without Authority — What 15 Years Taught Me About Influence

## The Meeting That Changed Everything

It was early 2022, and I was sitting in a virtual meeting room with 14 people across four countries. I'd just been given responsibility for a team of Advanced Cloud Experts spanning EMEA — architects in Germany, the UK, the Netherlands, France. Senior, experienced, brilliant people. Most of them had been doing this work longer than I'd been at Microsoft.

And none of them reported to me.

Not formally. Not yet. The org chart was still catching up with the reality on the ground. I had a mandate to build something — a cross-EMEA technical capability — but no reporting lines, no budget authority, and no history with most of these people. What I had was a shared Teams channel and a calendar invite.

I remember opening that first call with what I thought was a clear vision: here's where we're going, here's why it matters, here's how we'll get there. Confident. Structured. Slides and everything.

The silence afterwards was deafening.

Not hostile silence. Polite silence. The kind where people nod, say "sounds good," and then go back to exactly what they were doing before. I'd delivered a plan. What I hadn't earned was the right to be heard.

That was the moment I started learning what leading without authority actually means — and it wasn't what any leadership book had told me.

---

## The Uncomfortable Truth

Here's the thing I didn't want to admit at the time: I thought showing up with competence would be enough. I'd spent over a decade building technical depth — from racking servers at IT-Works to redesigning Active Directory at HUGO BOSS to architecting the largest Azure Virtual Desktop environments worldwide. I knew the technology cold.

But competence without connection is just noise.

Those first few weeks were humbling. I'd send detailed strategy documents. People would read them — I could see the read receipts — and nothing would change. I'd propose initiatives, and they'd die quietly in someone's backlog. I wasn't being undermined. I was being *politely ignored*.

The uncomfortable truth? I was leading like a broadcast tower — sending signals in all directions and wondering why nobody was tuning in. I hadn't taken the time to listen. To understand what each person on that team actually cared about, what problems kept them up at night, what success looked like in *their* context — not mine.

I was so focused on proving I deserved the role that I forgot the most basic thing: people don't follow plans. They follow people they trust.

---

## What Actually Worked

The turning point wasn't dramatic. There was no movie moment. It was a Thursday afternoon, and I called one of the senior architects in the UK — someone who'd been notably quiet on team calls. No agenda. No slides. I just asked: "What's the hardest thing on your plate right now?"

He talked for forty minutes.

He told me about a customer engagement that was falling apart, about internal processes that made his job harder, about ideas he'd been sitting on because nobody had asked. By the end of that call, I hadn't solved a single problem. But I'd done something more important — I'd shown up as someone who actually gave a damn.

I had that same conversation with every person on the team over the next three weeks. One by one. No template. Just listening.

And slowly, things shifted.

Not because I had new authority. Not because the org chart changed. But because the team started to see me as someone who was *in it with them* — not above them, not managing them from a dashboard, but actually invested in their success.

That team grew to 36 people. We built cross-EMEA initiatives that changed how Microsoft approached End-User Computing in the region. We ran technical bootcamps that reached over 1,200 Cloud Solution Architects. In 2025, the impact of that work was part of why I received the Microsoft Pinnacle Award — an award given to 43 people out of 228,000+ employees.

But I need to be honest: the award didn't come from having authority. It came from what happened in those messy, uncertain early days when I had none.

---

## Four Things I Learned the Hard Way

Looking back, I can distill 15 years of leading without authority into a few principles. But I want to be clear — I didn't learn these from a framework. I learned them from getting it wrong, adjusting, and trying again.

### 1. Credibility Is the Only Currency That Compounds

You can't fake your way through a room full of architects. They'll smell it immediately. But credibility isn't just about technical knowledge — it's about follow-through. Do what you said you'd do. Show up prepared. Admit when you don't know something.

I've found that saying "I don't know, but I'll find out" has earned me more trust than any polished presentation ever did.

### 2. Relationships Are Infrastructure

I think in systems — it's how my brain works. And over time, I started to see relationships the same way I see architecture: as infrastructure. They're the foundation everything else runs on. Without them, your brilliant strategy has no network to travel through.

Building relationships across four countries, different cultures, different working styles — that was the real engineering challenge. Not the Azure deployments.

### 3. Vision Without Listening Is Just a Monologue

That first meeting where I laid out my grand plan? It flopped because I was talking *at* people, not *with* them. A compelling vision matters — but it has to be shaped by the people who'll bring it to life.

The best ideas I've implemented weren't mine. They came from those one-on-one calls, from the architects in the field who were closer to the customer than I'd ever be. My job wasn't to have the vision. It was to create the space where the right vision could emerge.

### 4. Influence Is a Daily Practice, Not a Technique

There are books about "influence techniques" — reciprocity, social proof, all that. They're not wrong. But real influence isn't a tactic. It's the compound interest of showing up consistently, being honest even when it's awkward, and putting the team's success before your own visibility.

Some of the most influential moments in my career were the ones nobody saw: a quiet word of support before someone's big presentation, connecting two people who should've been talking months ago, taking the blame when something didn't land.

---

## What I'd Tell a Younger Version of Myself

If I could go back to that first meeting in 2022, I'd tell myself three things:

**Stop trying to prove yourself.** The harder you push for credibility, the more it slips away. Just do the work. Be useful. Be honest. Credibility follows.

**Get comfortable being uncomfortable.** Leading without authority means sitting with ambiguity. You won't always know if people are following. You won't always get credit. You won't always feel like a leader. That's normal. Do it anyway.

**Your job isn't to have answers — it's to ask better questions.** The most powerful thing you can do in a room full of experts is be genuinely curious about what they know that you don't.

---

## One More Thing

I want to acknowledge something I've learned to say out loud: I've had access and opportunity that not everyone gets. Being at Microsoft, working with the calibre of people I work with, having leaders who gave me room to grow — that's not a universal experience. The principles here are real, but the runway I had to learn them was privileged.

If you're in a position where you're trying to lead without authority *and* without much institutional support — that's harder. Genuinely harder. I don't want to pretend otherwise.

## The Invitation

Today, as a Technical Architect in the Innovation Hub, I still lead without formal authority every single day. I work across teams, across functions, across customer organisations. No one has to listen to me. The ones who do, do so because — I hope — they trust that I'm bringing something honest to the table.

Leading without authority isn't a career hack. It's not a skill you unlock at a certain level. It's a practice. A daily decision to show up, listen first, contribute genuinely, and trust that influence compounds over time.

If any of this resonates — or if you've learned different lessons on the same path — I'd love to hear your story. The best conversations I've had about leadership didn't happen on stage. They happened over coffee.

So consider this an open invitation. ☕
