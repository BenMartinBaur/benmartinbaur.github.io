# What Makes a Maker? The Pinnacle Effect and Habits That Scale

## The Calendar vs. The Outcome

I spent three months building something that looked impressive on paper but moved almost nothing in the organisation.

It was early 2023, and I'd been asked to design a standardised Azure governance framework for a new customer engagement. I was visible everywhere - in architecture reviews, presenting at leadership syncs, running working sessions across four different business units. My calendar was packed. My inbox was flooded. I felt important.

And exactly zero business processes changed.

Looking back, I realise what happened: I'd optimised for visibility instead of impact. The framework was solid - technically sound, well-documented, comprehensive. But it wasn't built with the teams who'd have to live with it. It was designed at them, not for them. So when I handed it off, it got filed away. Not rejected. Not critiqued. Just... filed.

Meanwhile, a quieter project was happening six floors down. A junior architect on the platform team had quietly started pairing with operations on a specific pain point - how long it took to provision new Azure subscriptions. No grand presentations. No task force. Just consistent, methodical problem-solving over six weeks.

That junior architect's work cut provisioning time from two weeks to three days. It became the standard across the company. That one pattern cascaded into every new engagement.

Here's the uncomfortable realisation: the person who got seen wasn't the person who moved anything. The person who moved the needle was the one nobody was watching.

This is the difference between activity and impact. And understanding it is everything for building a career that actually compounds.

## The Performative Visibility Trap

Enterprise tech is built for broadcast. Meeting culture. Status updates. Visibility metrics. "Make sure people know what you're doing." "You need executive presence." "Get in front of the right people."

All true. All also incomplete.

The trap is this: it's much easier to look like you're moving mountains than to actually move them. Visibility feels like progress. It tastes like success. But in large organisations, you can be incredibly visible and completely ineffectual - and sometimes you don't find out until the project dies or gets deprioritised.

I see this pattern constantly in my customer sessions. A team runs six workshops, produces a 40-page governance document, presents to the steering committee. Looks like progress. But then the work stalls because nobody agreed on which problem was actually being solved. The visibility was real. The alignment was fiction.

Here's what I learned the hard way: visible activity and measurable impact are orthogonal variables. You can have one without the other. The people who scale their careers understand this. They know that in a 3,000-person division or a 50,000-person enterprise, the people who change things are rarely the most visible people. They're the people who change things that other people then use.

The anti-pattern looks like this:
- Running the meeting instead of solving the problem
- Getting credit instead of sharing it
- Moving fast instead of moving right
- Optimising for a single audience (executives, peers, social media) instead of the people affected by your work

And the worst part? You can do it well. You can be genuinely competent AND fall into the visibility trap simultaneously. That's what makes it so dangerous.

## What Actually Compounds: Three Assets

I've been thinking about this wrong for years. I used to think career growth was about climbing titles or accumulating credentials. It's not. Career growth is about three compounding assets - and you can only build them by doing impact-driven work.

Asset 1: Credibility

This is what I called "compound currency" in my post on leading without authority. Credibility is the only asset that compounds reliably over time. It can't be faked. It can't be bought. It's built one delivery, one honest conversation, one follow-through at a time.

When people trust that you'll do what you say you'll do, that you think systemically, and that you care about solving the real problem - not the visible problem - you suddenly have options. Opportunities find you. Cross-functional partners want you on their projects. Leaders listen to what you propose.

But credibility only builds when you're focused on actual outcomes, not apparent progress.

Asset 2: Systems Thinking

This is the thing I've come to lean on most. When you spend years solving real problems - not busy work, not politically expedient solutions, but actual business problems - you start to see patterns across the organisation. You understand how decisions in one system ripple into others. You can predict where friction will occur.

Systems thinking isn't something you learn in a course. It's something you develop by consistently working through complicated problems and asking, "What else will this affect?" It becomes your operating model.

And here's why it compounds: once you develop it, you become disproportionately valuable. You're not just executing the task in front of you. You're anticipating second-order effects. You're connecting dots that other people haven't connected yet. You're thinking three moves ahead.

Asset 3: Influence Across Boundaries

I used to think influence came from title. It doesn't. Influence comes from trust that's distributed across multiple peer groups - not from a single line of authority.

The person who can move things across four different business units isn't moving them because they have authority in all four. They're moving them because people in all four have seen them solve real problems. Have seen them put the mission above the credit. Have seen them care about what works, not what looks good.

That kind of influence is only available to people who've consistently chosen impact over visibility.

## The Pinnacle Effect Filter: How to Know If You're Moving the Needle

How do you know if you're actually acting like a Maker? Here is the filter I run on my own work now - and I recommend it to anyone trying to build a career that scales:

| Question | What It Reveals |
|----------|-----------------|
| Did this change a business or customer outcome? | Moves visibility up in your priority list vs. visibility down. If you can't point to a specific outcome that changed, you're optimising for the wrong thing. |
| Can this approach be reused by others? | Separates one-off heroics from systematic improvement. Makers build habits and patterns. Busy people fight individual fires. |
| Does this increase trust across teams? | Measures whether people want to work with you again vs. whether they're just aware you exist. Trust is the only renewable resource. |

If the answer to all three is yes - congratulations, that is Pinnacle Effect-level work. If any are no, you might be trapped in visibility theatre.

## Exercise: Map Your Last 30 Days

Spend 15 minutes on this:

List your five most time-consuming activities from the last month. For each one, answer:

1. Who is directly affected by this work?
2. What outcome changes for them as a result?
3. Will this outcome compound or expire?
4. If I disappeared tomorrow, would this work continue creating value?

Look at your answers. How many of those five are visibility work vs. impact work? How many compound vs. expire?

This isn't judgment. This is data. Use it to recalibrate where you invest attention.
