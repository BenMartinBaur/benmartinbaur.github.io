# The First 1000 Days: How Credibility Actually Compounds

## The Project Nobody Wanted

It was 2009, and I was fresh out of early infrastructure work at IT-Works - maybe 18 months into my career. I'd been handed what looked like a dead-end assignment: migrate an internal helpdesk ticketing system from one platform to another.

Not sexy. Not visible. Not going to get me in front of executives.

Everyone on the team treated it like a box to check. Get it done. Move on. But I was young enough to take it seriously. I started documenting everything - not for show, but because the current system was chaos and I wanted to understand it. I talked to the helpdesk staff, actually listened to their pain points, and realised the real problem wasn't the platform migration. It was their workflow.

I spent my own time redesigning how tickets moved through the system. Built a small process improvement alongside the technical migration. Tested it with the team. Iterated based on their feedback.

When we cut over, something unexpected happened: ticket resolution time dropped 40%. Not a little. Not noticeable. Forty percent.

Here's the thing: nobody in leadership was watching this project. It wasn't on any exec's radar. I had zero visibility. And yet, it changed how I moved through the company for the next 15 years.

Because the helpdesk staff told their managers. Their managers told other teams. When I needed a complex infrastructure assessment done quickly, people said yes. When I had an idea that required multiple teams to collaborate, it happened. When I got hired to lead cross-functional initiatives, it was largely because - across the organisation - I'd built a reputation for actually solving problems instead of just managing them.

That one unglamorous project, done well, with nobody watching, taught me everything about how careers actually compound.

## Why the First Phase Is Mostly Invisible

Here's something they don't tell you early in your career: the most important work you do will be largely invisible.

Not to the people directly affected. But to the broader organisation. To the career marketers. To the LinkedIn crowd watching your trajectory.

In the first 1,000 days of a technical career - roughly the first two to three years - you're establishing a baseline operating discipline. You're learning how to see systems. You're building habits around how you approach problems. You're creating your first reference network.

And almost none of it looks impressive from the outside.

You're not leading transformations. You're not presenting at conferences. You're not shipping the flagship product. You're the person showing up, doing the work thoroughly, asking good questions, and following through on what you said you'd do.

It feels slow. It is slow. But this is the foundation everything else gets built on.

I see a lot of people in their first two years of tech trying to accelerate this phase. Looking for the headline project. The visible win. The thing that gets them noticed. And I get it - the pressure to prove yourself is real. But here's what I've learned: rushing the foundation phase is one of the most expensive mistakes you can make.

The people who skip ahead - who chase visibility too early, who try to leapfrog to leadership before they've built credibility - end up with a different problem later. They get opportunities based on optics rather than trust. When things get hard, they don't have a deep bench of people who believe in them. They exhaust themselves trying to maintain the appearance of competence instead of actually being competent.

The invisible work is the investment. The compound interest hasn't started yet.

## Headline Chasing: The Anti-Pattern That Stalls Your Growth

This is something I see constantly in high-performers early in their career, and it breaks my heart a little bit because I understand the impulse.

You're smart. You're ambitious. You want to move fast. So you optimise for wins that are observable. You chase projects that will look good on your next resume. You measure yourself against annual promotion cycles.

It seems logical. It's also a trap.

Here's why: observable wins and durable credibility are not the same thing. Sometimes they align. Often they don't. And if you optimise for the former, you'll find yourself three years into your career with impressive bullet points and zero real influence.

The headline-chasing pattern looks like:
- Volunteering for visible projects, avoiding complex foundational work
- Trying to get your contribution recognised publicly instead of letting the work speak for itself
- Changing roles frequently to jump to the next interesting thing instead of deepening expertise
- Optimising for individual achievement instead of team outcomes

And here's the kicker: you can execute all of this flawlessly and still end up in a blind alley. Because when the organisation needs someone who actually understands the system, who can fix things under pressure, who people trust - that person didn't get there by chasing headlines. They got there by doing the unglamorous work consistently.

One of my colleagues did this well. She spent her first two years at Microsoft documenting a massively overcomplicated deployment process. Unglamorous work. Nobody noticed. But when the process failed under production load, she was the only person who understood it well enough to fix it. That credibility got her into the room for the next strategic initiative. That led to a different opportunity. That led to another.

The other pattern I see? People who compile impressive headlines but never build real depth. They're always on the next project. They never stay long enough to see the consequences of their work. And when they get to a level where depth matters, they hit a ceiling.

## What Actually Compounds in the First 1000 Days

Let me map out the four things I've watched build careers that actually scale:

### 1. Shipping Discipline

This is the unglamorous superpower. The ability to move work from idea to completion. Not perfectly. Not always on time. But consistently.

Early in your career, building this habit changes everything downstream. You learn what it takes to actually finish something. You learn that good-enough-and-shipped beats perfect-and-perpetual. You develop the stamina for work that takes longer than a sprint.

People with strong shipping discipline get more opportunities because they're reliable. They're the person who delivers. Not the person with the most ideas. Not the person with the most visibility. The person who ships.

### 2. Documented Learning

I became obsessed early with writing down what I learned. Not publicly - I wasn't blogging in 2009. But in wikis, in runbooks, in architecture documents. Every time I solved a problem, I documented it.

This habit does three things:
- It forces clarity. If you can't explain it in writing, you don't actually understand it.
- It creates leverage. The next person doesn't have to learn it from scratch. That's influence without being in the room.
- It compounds. A year later, you can look back and see the progression of your own thinking.

Documented learning is the difference between solving a problem and solving a category of problems.

### 3. Asking Better Questions

I learned this from watching someone I respected early in my career. She rarely had the answer. But she asked questions that made you think differently about the problem.

In the first 1,000 days, you're not supposed to be the expert. You're building the habit of curiosity. Of understanding the system before trying to change it. Of asking "what's actually happening here?" instead of jumping to "here's what we should do."

This habit becomes your operating mode. Early on, it's about learning. Over time, it becomes about seeing patterns. Over a whole career, it becomes about wisdom.

### 4. Relationship Trust Across Roles

Here's what I realise now: that helpdesk ticketing project wasn't impressive because the project was impressive. It was impressive because I built real relationships with the people affected by it.

I cared about their experience. I asked them what was hard. I actually listened. I treated them like partners, not stakeholders.

Those relationships turned into a reference network. Not a political network - I wasn't collecting people strategically. I was building genuine trust with people across different roles and functions. And that trust is what opened doors over the next 15 years.

## The 1000-Day Compounding Map

Here's how to think about what you're building in your first phase:

| Dimension | How It Builds | What It Enables |
|-----------|---------------|-----------------|
| Skill Depth | Staying with problems long enough to understand their systems, not just the surface symptoms | You become the person people turn to for complex problems. You're not just familiar. You're trusted. |
| Reliability Signals | Following through consistently on small commitments. Saying "done" when done. Communicating when things change. | People start predicting that you'll deliver. They build strategies around your reliability. |
| Relationship Trust | Building real connections across functions. Listening more than pitching. Giving credit freely. | You develop influence that doesn't require authority. You become known as someone who makes teams better. |
| Reflection Loop | Regularly asking "what did I learn?" and "what would I do differently?" | You avoid repeating mistakes. Your growth rate accelerates. Others learn from your growth. |

## Exercise: Your Baseline Operating Model

Spend 15 minutes on this exercise:

Think back to the most unglamorous project you completed in your first year or two of technical work. The one that nobody was watching.

1. How thoroughly did you approach it? Did you understand the system, or just execute the task?
2. Who did you build relationships with? Did you treat them as stakeholders or partners?
3. What did you learn from it? Did you document that learning?
4. What happened to that work after you handed it off? Did it create ongoing value?

Now think about your current role. Which of those four compounding dimensions (skill depth, reliability, trust, reflection) are you investing in?

If you're not actively building at least two of them, you might be in headline-chasing mode instead of compounding mode. There's still time to shift.

## One Uncomfortable Truth

I want to name something: access matters. Having space to do thorough, deep work early in your career is a privilege. If you're in a role where you're constantly firefighting, where you're always under-resourced, where leadership doesn't value foundational work - that's genuinely harder.

The compound interest I'm describing requires a certain kind of environment. Not perfect. But one where you can actually finish projects and see their impact. If that's not your current situation, that's not a personal failure. That's a context failure.

I say that because I've had access that not everyone gets. And I don't want to pretend the compounding framework works the same way in all environments.

## The Next 10,000 Days

The first 1,000 days aren't about becoming a leader. They're about building the foundation that makes leadership possible. They're about developing habits that compound over decades.

Most people don't think about their career this way. They think in annual cycles. Annual reviews. Annual promotions. But the people who scale their impact think in decades. They know that small, consistent decisions early create enormous optionality later.

In the next post, we're going to talk about how that shifts - how your early habits eventually give you choices about the kind of impact you want to have.

But for now, here's the question I want to sit with you on: What are you building in the invisible phase that you won't see the payoff from for five years?

That's where your real work is.
