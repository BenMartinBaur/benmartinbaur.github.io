## Why "10x"?

The phrase "10x engineer" gets thrown around a lot in tech. Usually with eye-rolls. But I'm not using "10x" to describe a person. I'm using it to describe a **shift in impact surface area.**

When I moved into the role of Enterprise Architect in Microsoft's Innovation Hub — the Office of the CTO — the change wasn't about doing the same work better. It was about doing **fundamentally different work** with a fundamentally wider blast radius.

In my previous roles, I impacted individual deployments. Individual customers. Individual technologies.

In this role, I shape **end-to-end architecture and technology strategies for strategic customers across Germany**, with a strong focus on AI-driven transformation and platform thinking. The surface area of impact isn't 1x bigger. It's an order of magnitude shift.

This post is the first in a series where I'll share — honestly and in real time — what I'm learning. Not polished retrospectives. Real-time observations from someone navigating the transition from "really good at one thing" to "architecturally responsible for everything."

## What I Actually Do

Let me be concrete, because "Enterprise Architect in the Office of the CTO" sounds impressive and tells you nothing. Here's what the work actually looks like:

### 1. Customer-Back Innovation

I work **directly with customers** — not at a whiteboard theorizing about what they might need, but in their rooms, with their problems, under their constraints.

The approach:
- **Identify high-impact use cases** that create real business value (not tech demos)
- **Design transformation journeys** — not just architectures, but the path from where they are to where they need to be
- **Guide initiatives from envisioning through architecture design and validation** — from the first "what if?" conversation to a validated, implementable blueprint

This is "customer-back" because the architecture starts with the customer's reality — not with Microsoft's product catalog. The technology serves the outcome, not the other way around.

### 2. Voice of the Customer to Engineering

This is the part of the role I didn't fully appreciate until I was in it.

I sit at the intersection between **what customers actually need** and **what Microsoft Product Engineering builds**. My job is to:
- Amplify real customer needs — not anecdotes, but patterns
- Influence product roadmaps with signal from the field
- Ensure Microsoft outcomes are aligned with customer reality — not just aligned with internal metrics

This means I regularly collaborate with Microsoft's engineering teams, translating customer pain into product direction. When a customer's AI deployment hits a platform limitation, my job isn't just to work around it — it's to **ensure that limitation gets addressed at the product level** so no other customer hits it.

### 3. Cross-Solution Orchestration

Enterprise customers don't buy "Azure AI" or "Microsoft 365" in isolation. They need **cohesive, outcome-driven architectures** that bring together:
- Cloud infrastructure
- AI and machine learning
- Security and identity
- Data and analytics
- Modern workplace

My role is to **orchestrate across these solution areas** — designing architectures that scale beyond individual projects into enterprise-wide transformation. This is where platform thinking becomes essential: not building one solution at a time, but building the **platform** that enables hundreds of solutions.

### 4. Technical and Architectural Role Model

In the Innovation Hub, part of the mandate is to be a **role model** for what architectural excellence looks like. This means:
- Setting the standard for how we approach architecture with customers
- Creating repeatable AI transformation patterns and IP
- Contributing learnings back into the Innovation Hub and the broader Microsoft ecosystem
- Leading complex, cross-functional initiatives with clarity, alignment, and momentum

This connects directly to the principles from [my Pinnacle post](2026-04-02-the-0007-pinnacle-effect.md) — exceptional results and serving a unique customer experience aren't just nice-to-haves. They're the operating standard.

## The 30-60-90 Framework: What I've Learned

### Days 1-30: Listen. Absorb. Resist the Urge to "Add Value."

**The hardest lesson of the first 30 days: shut up and learn.**

Coming into the Innovation Hub with years of deep technical experience, my instinct was to immediately contribute. Add to conversations. Share opinions. Prove I belonged.

Instead, I forced myself to do something much harder: **listen.**

What I learned by listening:
- **The customer landscape is wider than I expected.** Strategic customers operate at a scale and complexity that makes my previous work feel like a sandbox. The architecture decisions have genuine P&L impact — millions, not thousands.
- **Context is everything.** An architecture pattern that works brilliantly for one customer can be disastrous for another. The difference is always context — organizational culture, existing technical debt, regulatory constraints, team capabilities.
- **Relationships precede architectures.** Before a customer will let you reshape their technology strategy, they need to trust you. Trust isn't earned by showing how smart you are. It's earned by showing how deeply you listen.

> Dr. Michael Gervais teaches that the foundation of high performance is a quieted mind. In the first 30 days, I practiced this literally: quieting my ego's need to contribute and letting the environment teach me.

**30-day principle**: *Earn the right to have an opinion by doing the work to understand the context.*

### Days 31-60: Pattern Recognition. Connect the Dots.

**The second month is where the 10x shift started clicking.**

With 30 days of active listening behind me, patterns started emerging:
- **The same 5 AI adoption blockers** appeared across nearly every strategic customer, regardless of industry
- **The architecture decisions that created the most customer value** weren't technology choices — they were decisions about data governance, identity, and change management
- **The gap between Microsoft's product vision and customer reality** was predictable and addressable — if someone was willing to name it honestly

This is the month where I started creating **repeatable IP**:

| Pattern | What I Observed | IP Created |
|---------|----------------|------------|
| AI readiness assessment | Customers couldn't articulate their AI maturity | A structured framework for evaluating AI readiness across 6 dimensions |
| Transformation journey mapping | Customers jumped to technology before understanding the journey | A visual journey map tool from current state to target architecture |
| Cross-solution architecture template | Every engagement reinvented the wheel | A reusable architecture canvas that spans cloud, AI, data, security, and workplace |
| Customer-back use case prioritization | Customers had 50 AI ideas and no way to rank them | A prioritization matrix balancing business impact, feasibility, and strategic alignment |

**60-day principle**: *Don't just solve problems. Recognize patterns and turn solutions into repeatable assets.*

### Days 61-90: Lead with Architecture. Shift the Conversation.

**The third month is where the work becomes truly architectural — and where this blog shifts too.**

By day 60, I had context. I had patterns. I had IP. Now came the harder challenge: **leading with architecture in conversations that weren't asking for it.**

Most customer engagements start with a question about technology: *"Should we use Azure OpenAI or deploy our own model?"* or *"How do we build a copilot for our employees?"*

The architectural move is to **reframe the question**:
- Not "which AI tool?" but "what's your AI platform strategy?"
- Not "how do we build this one thing?" but "how do we build the architecture that enables the next 100 things?"
- Not "what technology?" but "what outcome — and what architecture gets you there sustainably?"

This reframing is where the 10x impact lives. Because a technology answer solves one problem. An architecture answer **creates a capability that solves a category of problems.**

**90-day principle**: *Technology is the answer to "what." Architecture is the answer to "how many times, how sustainably, how scalably."*

## My Guiding Principles for This Role

Every role needs an operating philosophy. These are the principles I've committed to — written down, reviewed weekly, non-negotiable:

### 1. Deliver Exceptional Results

Not good results. Not above-average results. **Exceptional.**

This means every customer interaction, every architecture artifact, every internal collaboration reflects the highest standard I'm capable of. Not because someone is measuring — but because the standard is who I am.

### 2. Serve a Unique Customer Experience

Every customer should walk away from an engagement with me feeling like they received something they couldn't get anywhere else. Not because of the technology — because of the **depth of understanding, the quality of thinking, and the genuine care for their outcome.**

This is the difference between a vendor and a trusted advisor. Vendors deliver products. Trusted advisors deliver **clarity about the path forward.**

### 3. Be the Architectural Role Model

In the Innovation Hub, I don't just practice architecture — I set the standard for how it's practiced. This means:
- My work is shareable and teachable (not locked in my head)
- My approaches are repeatable (not heroic one-offs)
- My interactions model the behavior I expect from others

### 4. Bridge Customer Reality and Product Vision

I refuse to sell a vision that doesn't connect to the customer's reality. And I refuse to accept customer limitations without challenging whether Microsoft's products can evolve to meet them. This bridge — honest in both directions — is where the real value lives.

## The Vulnerability Moment

I want to be honest about something.

This role is **intimidating.** The customers are more senior. The problems are more complex. The stakes are higher. The expectations — from Microsoft, from customers, from myself — are at a level I haven't operated at before.

There are days when I sit in a meeting with a customer's CTO and think: *"Am I really the right person for this?"*

And then I remember something Toto Wolff said about his own self-doubt: he's been in therapy for over 500 hours, has struggled for months without a clear thought, and calls mental well-being his "super-power." If the man who led eight consecutive F1 championships wrestles with doubt, maybe doubt isn't the disease — it's the signal that you're operating at your edge.

> Gervais would call this the frontier of growth: *"We have the power to dictate our own inner experience."* The doubt isn't going away. The question is whether I let it drive me to shrink — or to prepare more deeply.

I choose preparation.
