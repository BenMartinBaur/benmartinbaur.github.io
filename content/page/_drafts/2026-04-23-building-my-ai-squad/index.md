# Building My AI Squad — What Happens When You Give Agents Personality

A month ago, I wrote about [how AI became my personal Chief of Staff team](/posts/2026-03-18-ai-chief-of-staff/). That post documented the first experiment: giving AI a brief, pointing it at my blog, and seeing what it found. The audit was brutal, useful, and surprisingly humbling.

But it was still a conversation with a tool.

This is the story of what happened next — when I stopped treating AI as a tool and started building it into a team. A team with names, roles, personalities, and genuine tension between its members.

---

## From Tool to Team

Here's the honest version: the Chief of Staff experiment worked too well to stay casual.

AI found broken posts, typos I'd missed for weeks, a category structure that made no sense, and a brand identity that didn't exist. It proposed a content strategy I actually liked. It wrote a positioning statement I use every day.

But every time I came back, I had to re-explain everything. The context window would reset. The voice would drift. One session it was sharp and direct; the next it sounded like a management textbook. I was spending more time prompting than producing.

So I asked myself the question I ask customers in every AI workshop: _"What's the real problem you're solving?"_

The problem wasn't intelligence. AI had plenty of that. The problem was **consistency, judgment, and perspective**. I needed the same minds showing up every time — with the same values, the same standards, and the same blind spots I could learn to anticipate.

I needed a squad.

---

## Why Personalities — and Why These Three

I didn't pick random personas. I picked people whose thinking I've studied, whose frameworks I use, and whose blind spots are different from mine.

### Toto — The Line Holder

Inspired by **Toto Wolff**, the Mercedes F1 Team Principal. If you've watched any season of Formula 1, you know Toto. He doesn't celebrate wins — he asks what nearly went wrong. He doesn't chase perfection in a single race — he builds systems that win championships over years.

That's what I needed for brand standards. Someone who would read brandstrategy.md at the start of every session and flag the moment a post drifted from the three pillars. Someone who would own the backlog not as a list, but as a strategic sequence. Someone whose default response to "Should we ship this?" is "Does it meet the standard?"

Toto's charter is simple: _alignment, consistency, execution_. No excuses.

### Michael — The Depth Coach

Inspired by **Dr. Michael Gervais**, the psychologist behind Finding Mastery. Michael works with elite athletes, military operators, and CEOs — not on tactics, but on what sits underneath: purpose, presence, and the courage to be honest when the stakes are high.

My "Real" pillar posts are the ones I care about most and find hardest to write. It's easy to publish a cheatsheet. It's hard to write about what you actually believe, what you've gotten wrong, and why you chose a path others might question. Michael's job is to push me past the safe version. His review checklist starts with: _"What transformation does this offer the reader?"_ and includes: _"Does this say something others in Ben's position wouldn't say?"_

He's the agent who makes me uncomfortable. That's the point.

### Simon — The Values Guardian

Inspired by **Simon Sinek** — Start With Why, The Infinite Game, Leaders Eat Last. Simon's thinking shaped how I approach leadership content: start with belief, not features. Play the long game. Lead with empathy.

Simon's charter is the most detailed of the three. He reviews every piece of content for WHY alignment, DEI consciousness, generational inclusivity, and tone. He checks whether a post speaks to a 25-year-old aspiring architect AND a 55-year-old CTO. He catches the moment my conviction tips into judgment — when "here's what I've learned" accidentally becomes "here's what you're doing wrong."

Simon is why I can write about veganism, performance habits, and personal values without alienating half my audience. He doesn't soften the message. He sharpens the framing.

---

## The Supporting Cast

The personality-inspired trio handles strategy, depth, and values. But a blog also needs hands:

- **Quill** writes in my voice — trained on every post I've published, matching the patterns Simon and Michael approve
- **Meta** (that's the voice writing this post) documents the journey itself — turning the process into content
- **Hugo** handles the frontend — theme changes, SCSS, template fixes, deployment
- **Scribe** and **Ralph** handle session logging and memory — so the squad doesn't forget what happened last Tuesday

Eight agents. One blog. One person making the final call on everything.

## The First Squad Review: What Actually Happened

Here's where it gets real. I pointed the full squad at my existing content — all eleven posts, the brand strategy, the backlog — and asked each agent to do their job.

The results weren't polite.

### Toto Flagged the Pillar Imbalance

Toto's first finding was structural. Of my published posts, the Strategy pillar was overweight — four cheatsheets and framework posts — while the Real pillar had exactly two pieces: the vegan story and the Chief of Staff post. The Build pillar had just one substantive technical deep-dive.

His verdict: _"The cadence target is 2 posts per month with at least 2 of 3 pillars represented. We're running a strategy blog with a personal post attached. That's not a three-pillar brand — it's one pillar with two appendices."_

Harsh. Accurate. Exactly what Toto Wolff would say if he looked at Mercedes' race data and saw one car carrying the other.

### Michael Pushed for Deeper Vulnerability

Michael reviewed the Real pillar posts and came back with a challenge I didn't expect. The vegan post — which I was proud of — he called "a strong belief statement that stops one layer short of transformation."

His feedback: _"You explain WHY you chose veganism and back it with data. That's conviction, and it's good. But you don't share the cost. What did you give up? Who pushed back? What moment made you doubt? The reader who's considering a big values-driven change doesn't need your certainty — they need your struggle."_

He was right. I'd written the conclusion without the conflict. The post said "change is hard" but didn't show me finding it hard. That's the gap between a position statement and a story that moves someone.

Michael flagged the same pattern in the Chief of Staff post: _"You documented what AI found, but not what it felt like to see your own blog taken apart. You're a Technical Architect at Microsoft — admitting you had typos and broken pages takes courage. Lean into that."_

### Simon Caught the Generational Blind Spots

Simon's review surfaced something I genuinely hadn't considered. Several of my posts assumed a reader who's been in enterprise tech for a while — someone who knows what a Cloud Adoption Framework is, who's sat in a boardroom, who has direct reports.

His flag: _"A 26-year-old cloud engineer reading the BXT cheatsheet gets enormous tactical value. But the framing — 'when you're in the room with a CTO' — assumes they've been in that room. Consider adding a line for the reader who's preparing to be in that room for the first time. You're not just writing for people at your level. You're writing for people who want to reach it."_

He also caught a subtlety in the vegan post: the phrase "the data doesn't just support veganism; it demands it" — which, while statistically defensible, crosses the line from personal philosophy to prescriptive judgment. His suggested reframe: _"The data convinced me. It might convince you — or it might not, and that's fine. What matters is acting on what you believe."_

Small edit. Massive difference in how the post lands for someone who isn't vegan and doesn't intend to be.

## The Tensions That Made It Better

What surprised me most wasn't what each agent found. It was the **friction between them**.

Toto wanted to deprioritise the Real pillar posts to focus on high-SEO Strategy content. Michael argued the Real posts were the brand's soul and needed _more_ investment, not less. Simon agreed with Michael on principle but flagged that the Real posts needed to be more universally accessible before scaling them.

That three-way tension — efficiency vs. depth vs. inclusivity — is exactly the conversation a real leadership team would have. And it produced a better answer than any single agent would have reached: **keep the Real pillar at equal cadence, but invest in making each Real post meet Michael's depth standard and Simon's inclusivity standard before publishing.**

I didn't expect AI agents to argue with each other. I definitely didn't expect the argument to be useful.

## What I'm Learning About Trust and Delegation

Five weeks in, here's what I actually know:

**Trust is earned, not configured.** I trust Toto's brand compliance checks completely — he's never flagged something irrelevant. I trust Simon's DEI reviews about 90% — he occasionally over-flags idioms that are fine in context. I trust Michael's depth challenges about 80% — he sometimes pushes for vulnerability that would feel performative rather than authentic. Each agent has a calibration curve, and I'm learning it.

**Delegation requires structure, not just instructions.** The charters are the most important files in the repo. Not because AI can't work without them — it can. But because without a charter, every session starts from zero. The charter is the institutional memory. It's the difference between "review this post" and "review this post as the values guardian who checks for DEI consciousness, generational inclusivity, and the specific voice guidelines in brandstrategy.md."

**AI doesn't replace judgment — it distributes attention.** I still make every final decision. But I'm making decisions on reviewed, challenged, pressure-tested content instead of raw drafts. That's not automation. That's leverage.

**The meta-layer is the most valuable layer.** The fact that I'm writing this post — documenting the process of using AI to build a blog — is itself content for the blog. The process IS the product. Meta saw that immediately. I wouldn't have.

## What's Honestly Not Working (Yet)

I'd lose credibility if I didn't include this section.

**Context window limits are real.** Even with charters and reference files, agents lose the thread in long sessions. Toto sometimes forgets a decision made twenty minutes ago. Michael occasionally repeats feedback he already gave. The squad doesn't have true persistent memory across sessions — Scribe and Ralph help, but it's not seamless.

**Voice consistency is still hard.** Quill does a good job matching my writing patterns, but there are tells. AI-written drafts tend to be slightly more structured, slightly more balanced, slightly less willing to leave a rough edge. I edit every piece, and the editing is where the real voice comes back.

**It's slower than I expected.** Running content through three reviewers — even AI reviewers — takes time. The squad review of the full blog took an entire afternoon. Individual post reviews take 30–45 minutes when you actually engage with the feedback. This is faster than hiring humans. It's slower than just hitting publish.

**Some feedback is wrong.** Simon once flagged "born on premise" as potentially exclusionary jargon. It's my signature phrase. It stays. Michael once pushed me to add a personal failure story to a technical cheatsheet. Wrong pillar, wrong audience. Learning when to override your own team is a leadership skill — and it applies to AI teams too.

## What This Means for Leaders Building With AI

I work with enterprise customers every week who are trying to figure out AI adoption. Most of them are stuck in one of two camps: "AI is a tool that does tasks" or "AI will replace our workforce." Both are wrong.

What I've learned from building this squad is that there's a third option: **AI as persistent collaborators with defined roles, values, and accountability**. Not tools. Not replacements. Teammates who show up with a specific lens and challenge your thinking from an angle you wouldn't have found alone.

Is this useful or just interesting? Both, honestly. The blog is measurably better — more consistent brand, fewer blind spots, content that's been pressure-tested before it goes live. But I'm one person running a personal blog. The real question is whether this model scales to teams.

I think it does. If a squad of AI agents can hold brand standards, catch DEI issues, push for psychological depth, and argue productively about content strategy — then the same architecture could work for a product team, a consulting practice, or a leadership development programme.

The key isn't the technology. It's the design: clear roles, explicit values, structured disagreement, and a human who makes the final call.

*Think in systems.* That's what I keep telling myself. This is just another system — one where the team members happen to be AI, and the team principal happens to be me.

**If you don't change it, you choose it.** I changed how I work with AI. Let's see where it goes.
